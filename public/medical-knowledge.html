Diseases Directory

Access authoritative, peer-reviewed clinical summaries for viral infections, genetic disorders, chronic illnesses, and autoimmune diseases worldwide.

Alzheimer's Disease

Neurological

A progressive neurodegenerative disorder that destroys memory and key mental functions.

Symptoms

Persistent memory loss, confusion with time/place, difficulty solving simple problems, and personality changes.

Causes

Abnormal accumulation of amyloid plaques and neurofibrillary tangles in brain tissue, leading to cell death.

Precautions

Engage in regular physical exercise, maintain cardiovascular health (low BP/cholesterol), avoid head trauma, and limit alcohol and tobacco.

Cure & Treatment

No Cure. Treatment focuses on managing symptoms using Cholinesterase Inhibitors (Donepezil) and NMDA receptor antagonists (Memantine), combined with cognitive therapy.

Bronchitis

Respiratory

Inflammation of the mucous membrane in the bronchial tubes, causing bronchial spasms and coughing.

Symptoms

Persistent cough, production of thick mucus, shortness of breath, fatigue, and minor chest discomfort.

Causes

Usually caused by viral infections (similar to cold/flu viruses) or chronic exposure to irritants like tobacco smoke.

Precautions

Avoid cigarette smoke and air pollutants, wash hands frequently, wear a mask in dusty areas, and stay hydrated.

Cure & Treatment

Curable (Acute). Acute bronchitis resolves on its own. Treatments include rest, fluids, cough suppressants, and bronchodilators (Albuterol). Antibiotics are only used if a bacterial infection is proven.

Cystic Fibrosis

Genetic Disorder

An inherited life-threatening disorder that damages the lungs and digestive system by creating thick, sticky mucus.

Symptoms

Salty-tasting skin, persistent coughing with thick spit, frequent lung infections, wheezing, and poor growth/weight gain.

Causes

Mutations in the CFTR gene, inherited in an autosomal recessive pattern, which disrupts salt and water balance in cells.

Precautions

Practice strict hygiene to prevent infections, avoid contact with sick individuals, keep up-to-date with vaccinations, and avoid dust/smoke.

Cure & Treatment

No Cure. Managed using CFTR Modulator drugs (e.g., Trikafta) to improve protein function, airway clearance therapies (chest physiotherapy), inhaled bronchodilators, and pancreatic enzyme supplements.

Dengue Fever

Viral Infection

A painful, mosquito-borne viral disease causing severe flu-like symptoms and joint pain.

Symptoms

Sudden high fever, severe headache, pain behind the eyes, severe joint and muscle pain ("breakbone fever"), rash, and mild bleeding.

Causes

Dengue virus transmitted to humans through the bite of infected female Aedes aegypti mosquitoes.

Precautions

Eliminate standing water near the home, use mosquito repellents (DEET), sleep under insecticide-treated bed nets, and wear long-sleeved clothing.

Cure & Treatment

Curable / Self-Limiting. No specific antiviral cure. Management is supportive: intensive oral rehydration, pain relief using Acetaminophen (never use Aspirin or Ibuprofen as they increase bleeding risk), and platelet count monitoring.

Epilepsy

Neurological

A central nervous system disorder in which brain activity becomes abnormal, causing seizures.

Symptoms

Temporary confusion, staring spells, uncontrollable jerking movements of arms and legs, loss of consciousness, and anxiety.

Causes

Genetic influence, head trauma, brain conditions (stroke, tumors), prenatal injury, or developmental disorders.

Precautions

Avoid known seizure triggers (flashing lights, sleep deprivation, extreme stress), take prescribed medications consistently, and secure physical surroundings to prevent injury during a seizure.

Cure & Treatment

Sometimes Curable / Manageable. Often managed successfully with anti-seizure medications (AEDs like Levetiracetam, Lamotrigine). In some cases, epilepsy can be cured through surgical resection of the brain's seizure focus, or it may resolve spontaneously (especially childhood-onset epilepsy).

Fibromyalgia

Rheumatological

A disorder characterized by widespread musculoskeletal pain accompanied by fatigue, sleep, memory, and mood issues.

Symptoms

Widespread dull ache lasting at least 3 months, chronic fatigue, cognitive difficulties ("fibro fog"), and sleep disturbances.

Causes

Believed to involve abnormal pain signaling in the brain and spinal cord, often triggered by physical trauma, surgery, or psychological stress.

Precautions

Maintain a consistent sleep schedule, practice stress-reduction techniques (meditation, yoga), and engage in regular, low-impact exercise (walking, swimming).

Cure & Treatment

No Cure. Treatment aims to manage symptoms using pain relievers (Pregabalin, Duloxetine), low-dose antidepressants, physical therapy, and cognitive behavioral therapy (CBT) to improve coping mechanisms.

Gout

Metabolic / Joint

A common and complex form of arthritis characterized by sudden, severe attacks of pain and redness in joints.

Symptoms

Intense joint pain (typically in the big toe), lingering discomfort, inflammation, redness, and limited range of motion.

Causes

Accumulation of urate crystals in the joint, caused by high levels of uric acid (hyperuricemia) in the blood.

Precautions

Limit alcohol consumption (especially beer), avoid high-purine foods (red meat, seafood, organ meats), drink plenty of water, and avoid rapid weight loss diets which can spike uric acid.

Cure & Treatment

Highly Curable & Manageable. Acute attacks are cured/resolved using NSAIDs (Indomethacin), Colchicine, or Corticosteroids. Long-term recurrences are prevented and cured by lowering blood uric acid levels with daily medications like Allopurinol or Febuxostat.

Huntington's Disease

Genetic Disorder

An inherited, progressive brain disorder that causes the uncontrolled breakdown of nerve cells in the brain.

Symptoms

Involuntary jerking movements (chorea), muscle rigidity, difficulty speaking or swallowing, cognitive decline, and psychiatric changes.

Causes

An autosomal dominant mutation in the HTT gene, characterized by abnormal CAG triplet repeats.

Precautions

Ensure fall prevention measures are installed in the home, utilize speech therapy early to prevent choking, and undergo genetic screening if there is a family history.

Cure & Treatment

No Cure. Treatment is supportive and focuses on managing symptoms: Tetrabenazine or Deutetrabenazine to reduce involuntary movements (chorea), antipsychotic medications for mood disturbances, and physical therapy.

Inflammatory Bowel Disease

Gastrointestinal

An umbrella term describing chronic inflammation of the digestive tract, including Crohn's and Ulcerative Colitis.

Symptoms

Persistent diarrhea, abdominal pain and cramping, blood in stool, unexplained weight loss, fatigue, and reduced appetite.

Causes

Immune system malfunction where the body attacks its own digestive tissues, triggered by environmental factors and genetics.

Precautions

Avoid trigger foods (spicy, high-fat, or high-fiber foods during flares), eat smaller meals, drink plenty of water, and avoid NSAID pain relievers which can worsen inflammation.

Cure & Treatment

No Cure (Highly Manageable). Treatment aims for long-term remission using anti-inflammatory drugs (Aminosalicyclates, Corticosteroids), immunomodulators (Azathioprine), and biologic therapies (Infliximab). In severe cases of Ulcerative Colitis, surgical removal of the colon (colectomy) is considered a curative procedure.

Joint Rheumatoid Arthritis

Autoimmune

A chronic inflammatory disorder where the immune system mistakenly attacks the lining of the joints.

Symptoms

Tender, warm, swollen joints, joint stiffness (especially in the morning), fatigue, fever, and loss of appetite.

Causes

An autoimmune reaction where the body's immune system attacks the synovium, the membrane surrounding joints, destroying cartilage and bone.

Precautions

Protect joints from heavy stress, maintain a healthy weight, perform gentle range-of-motion exercises, and avoid smoking, which increases disease severity.

Cure & Treatment

No Cure. The goal is to induce clinical remission. Treatment involves early and aggressive use of Disease-Modifying Antirheumatic Drugs (DMARDs like Methotrexate), Biologics (TNF inhibitors), and NSAIDs for pain control, alongside physical therapy.

Kidney Disease (Chronic)

Renal

A gradual loss of kidney function over time, leading to dangerous levels of fluid and waste in the body.

Symptoms

Nausea, vomiting, loss of appetite, fatigue, sleep problems, changes in urine output, swelling of feet and ankles, and hypertension.

Causes

Most commonly caused by diabetes and high blood pressure, which damage the delicate filtering units (nephrons) in the kidneys.

Precautions

Strictly control blood sugar and blood pressure, follow a low-sodium and low-protein diet, stay hydrated (unless advised otherwise), and avoid nephrotoxic drugs (like NSAIDs).

Cure & Treatment

Irreversible (Manageable). Damage cannot be undone. Treatment focuses on slowing progression using ACE inhibitors or ARBs for blood pressure, dietary changes, and managing underlying causes. End-stage renal disease (ESRD) requires dialysis or a kidney transplant.

Lupus Erythematosus

Autoimmune

A systemic autoimmune disease that occurs when your body's immune system attacks your own tissues and organs.

Symptoms

Fatigue, fever, joint pain and stiffness, a butterfly-shaped rash on the face, skin lesions, shortness of breath, and chest pain.

Causes

Autoimmune response triggered by a combination of genetics and environmental factors (sunlight, infections, medications).

Precautions

Avoid direct sun exposure (wear SPF 50+ and protective clothing), get adequate rest, exercise regularly, and avoid known physical or emotional stressors.

Cure & Treatment

No Cure. Symptoms are managed and flares prevented using Hydroxychloroquine (antimalarial), Corticosteroids (Prednisone) for acute inflammation, and Immunosuppressants (Mycophenolate, Azathioprine).

Malaria

Parasitic Disease

A serious and sometimes fatal disease caused by a parasite that commonly infects a certain type of mosquito.

Symptoms

Fever, chills, headache, sweats, fatigue, nausea, vomiting, muscle aches, and bloody stools.

Causes

Plasmodium parasite transmitted through the bite of an infected female Anopheles mosquito.

Precautions

Use insecticide-treated bed nets, spray indoor areas with insect repellent, wear protective clothing, and take prophylactic antimalarial drugs when traveling to high-risk regions.

Cure & Treatment

Fully Curable. If diagnosed early, malaria can be completely cured. Treatment consists of prescription antimalarial drugs, primarily Artemisinin-based Combination Therapies (ACTs), Chloroquine, or Atovaquone-Proguanil, depending on parasite resistance.

Narcolepsy

Neurological

A chronic sleep disorder characterized by overwhelming daytime drowsiness and sudden attacks of sleep.

Symptoms

Excessive daytime sleepiness, sudden loss of muscle tone (cataplexy), sleep paralysis, and hallucinations.

Causes

A deficiency of hypocretin (orexin), a chemical in the brain that regulates wakefulness, often due to an autoimmune attack.

Precautions

Maintain a strict, consistent sleep schedule, take short, scheduled naps throughout the day, avoid heavy carbohydrate meals before important activities, and avoid driving if sleepiness is uncontrolled.

Cure & Treatment

No Cure. Symptoms are managed with stimulants (Modafinil, Armodafinil) or amphetamine-like drugs to combat sleepiness, and Sodium Oxybate or antidepressants (SSRIs) to treat cataplexy and sleep paralysis.

Osteogenesis Imperfecta

Genetic Disorder

An inherited genetic bone disorder, also known as brittle bone disease, characterized by fragile bones that break easily.

Symptoms

Bones that fracture easily, bone pain, blue tint in the whites of the eyes (sclera), early hearing loss, and short stature.

Causes

Mutations in the COL1A1 or COL1A2 genes, which are responsible for producing type I collagen, a structural protein in bones.

Precautions

Avoid high-impact sports or activities with a high fall risk, maintain a safe home environment (remove tripping hazards), and perform low-impact exercises like swimming to maintain muscle strength without overloading bones.

Cure & Treatment

No Cure. Treatment focuses on preventing fractures and managing symptoms: Bisphosphonate drugs (Pamidronate, Zoledronic acid) to increase bone density, physical therapy, orthopedic surgery (inserting metal rods into long bones), and mobility aids.

Parkinson's Disease

Neurological

A progressive nervous system disorder that affects movement, starting with a barely noticeable tremor.

Symptoms

Tremors (often in hands), slowed movement (bradykinesia), rigid muscles, impaired posture and balance, and speech changes.

Causes

Loss of dopamine-producing neurons in the substantia nigra area of the brain, leading to impaired motor signaling.

Precautions

Remove home hazards to prevent falls (install grab bars, remove throw rugs), use assistive walking devices if needed, and practice speech and physical therapy exercises daily.

Cure & Treatment

No Cure. Symptoms are managed using Levopoda/Carbidopa (which converts to dopamine in the brain), Dopamine Agonists (Pramipexole), MAO-B inhibitors, and in advanced cases, Deep Brain Stimulation (DBS) surgery.

Q Fever

Infectious Disease

A bacterial infection caused by Coxiella burnetii, primarily associated with livestock and agricultural workers.

Symptoms

High fever, severe headache, fatigue, chills, muscle aches, nausea, chest pain, and pneumonia-like cough.

Causes

Inhalation of dust contaminated with birth fluids, feces, urine, or milk of infected animals (sheep, goats, cattle).

Precautions

Avoid consuming unpasteurized milk and dairy products, wear protective masks and gloves when handling livestock or during animal birth, and disinfect animal housing areas regularly.

Cure & Treatment

Fully Curable. Acute Q Fever is successfully cured with a 2-week course of Doxycycline antibiotics. Chronic Q Fever (rare but serious) is treated with a combination of Doxycycline and Hydroxychloroquine for at least 18 months.

Rheumatoid Arthritis

Autoimmune

A systemic inflammatory disease that can affect more than just your joints, damaging skin, eyes, and lungs.

Symptoms

Symmetrical joint swelling, morning stiffness, rheumatoid nodules under the skin, fatigue, and weight loss.

Causes

An autoimmune process where the immune system attacks the joint linings. Triggered by genetic factors and smoking.

Precautions

Avoid repetitive joint strain, stay physically active with low-impact workouts, eat an anti-inflammatory diet (omega-3 rich), and avoid smoking.

Cure & Treatment

No Cure. Managed effectively using disease-modifying antirheumatic drugs (DMARDs like Methotrexate) and biologic agents to halt joint destruction, along with NSAIDs or low-dose steroids for pain.

Sickle Cell Anemia

Genetic Disorder

An inherited red blood cell disorder in which there aren't enough healthy red blood cells to carry oxygen throughout your body.

Symptoms

Episodes of severe pain (pain crises), swelling of hands and feet, frequent infections, delayed growth, and vision problems.

Causes

An autosomal recessive mutation in the HBB gene, causing red blood cells to become rigid, sticky, and sickle-shaped.

Precautions

Stay extremely well-hydrated, avoid sudden temperature changes (which can trigger a crisis), avoid strenuous high-altitude activities, and seek immediate medical attention for any fever.

Cure & Treatment

Curable Only via Transplant. The only cure is a bone marrow or stem cell transplant, typically reserved for severe cases in children due to high risks. Otherwise, treatment is symptomatic: Hydroxyurea to reduce pain crises, pain medications, vaccinations, and blood transfusions.

Tuberculosis

Infectious Disease

A serious infectious disease that mainly affects your lungs, spreading through tiny droplets released into the air.

Symptoms

Coughing that lasts three or more weeks, coughing up blood, chest pain, unintentional weight loss, fatigue, fever, and night sweats.

Causes

Infection by Mycobacterium tuberculosis bacteria, transmitted through respiratory droplets from infected individuals.

Precautions

Avoid close contact with active TB patients in unventilated spaces, wear N95 respirators in high-risk clinical environments, and complete latent TB treatments if exposed.

Cure & Treatment

Fully Curable. Active tuberculosis is cured with a strict 6-to-9-month regimen of multiple antibiotics (Isoniazid, Rifampin, Ethambutol, and Pyrazinamide). Compliance is critical to prevent drug-resistant TB strains.

Ulcerative Colitis

Gastrointestinal

An inflammatory bowel disease that causes long-lasting inflammation and ulcers in the innermost lining of your large intestine.

Symptoms

Diarrhea, often with blood or pus, abdominal pain and cramping, rectal pain, urgency to defecate, and weight loss.

Causes

Immune system dysfunction where it attacks the colon, influenced by genetic susceptibility and environmental triggers.

Precautions

Maintain a food diary to identify trigger foods, eat smaller, more frequent meals, limit caffeine and alcohol, and manage stress through relaxation techniques.

Cure & Treatment

Surgically Curable. There is no medical cure, but symptoms are managed with anti-inflammatory drugs (Sulfasalazine, Mesalamine), corticosteroids, and biologics. Surgical removal of the entire colon and rectum (proctocolectomy) is considered curative and eliminates the disease.

Vertigo

Neurological

A sensation of spinning or dizziness, often caused by inner ear problems or neurological conditions.

Symptoms

Spinning sensation, tilting, swaying, unbalance, nausea, vomiting, abnormal eye movements (nystagmus), and headache.

Causes

Benign paroxysmal positional vertigo (BPPV), Meniere's disease, vestibular neuritis, or central brain stem/cerebellar issues.

Precautions

Avoid sudden head movements or bending over, sit down immediately when dizziness strikes, ensure hallways are well-lit, and avoid driving or operating machinery during episodes.

Cure & Treatment

Highly Curable. BPPV is cured using specific physical canalith repositioning maneuvers (like the Epley Maneuver). Other forms are treated with vestibular suppressant medications (Meclizine), physical therapy, or treating the underlying infection.

Wilson's Disease

Genetic Disorder

A rare inherited disorder that causes copper to accumulate in your liver, brain, and other vital organs.

Symptoms

Fatigue, jaundice, golden-brown eye discoloration (Kayser-Fleischer rings), speech/coordination problems, and muscle stiffness.

Causes

Autosomal recessive mutations in the ATP7B gene, which prevents the body from excreting excess copper through bile.

Precautions

Strictly avoid foods high in copper (chocolate, mushrooms, shellfish, nuts, organ meats), have home tap water tested for copper content, and undergo routine liver function monitoring.

Cure & Treatment

Highly Treatable / Preventable. No genetic cure, but copper accumulation is fully controlled and damage prevented. Treatment requires lifelong therapy using chelating agents (Penicillamine, Trientine) to bind and remove copper, or Zinc Acetate to prevent copper absorption.

X-Linked Agammaglobulinemia

Genetic Disorder

An inherited primary immunodeficiency disease characterized by the inability to produce mature B cells and antibodies.

Symptoms

Frequent, severe bacterial infections (bronchitis, pneumonia, sinusitis, ear infections) beginning in infancy, and chronic diarrhea.

Causes

Mutations in the BTK gene on the X chromosome, preventing B lymphocyte development.

Precautions

Maintain exceptional personal hygiene, avoid crowds and sick individuals, consume only fully cooked foods, and avoid receiving live-virus vaccines (which can cause disease in immunodeficient individuals).

Cure & Treatment

No Cure. The primary treatment is lifelong antibody replacement therapy using Intravenous or Subcutaneous Immunoglobulin (IVIG/SCIG) infusions every 3 to 4 weeks to provide passive immunity and prevent infections.

Yellow Fever

Viral Infection

An acute viral hemorrhagic disease transmitted by infected mosquitoes, causing jaundice and kidney failure.

Symptoms

Fever, headache, jaundice (yellowing of skin/eyes), muscle pain, nausea, vomiting, and fatigue. Can progress to severe hemorrhaging.

Causes

Yellow fever virus transmitted by the bite of infected Aedes or Haemagogus mosquitoes.

Precautions

Obtain the highly effective Yellow Fever Vaccine (single dose provides lifelong protection), use mosquito repellents, and eliminate mosquito breeding sites.

Cure & Treatment

No Specific Cure. Treatment is purely supportive, aimed at relieving symptoms: hospitalization, oral or intravenous rehydration, and medication to manage fever and pain (avoiding Aspirin or NSAIDs due to bleeding risks).

Zika Virus

Viral Infection

A mosquito-borne viral disease that can cause congenital brain abnormalities, including microcephaly, in newborns.

Symptoms

Mild fever, rash, conjunctivitis (red eyes), muscle and joint pain, headache, and malaise lasting 2-7 days.

Causes

Zika virus transmitted primarily by the bite of an infected Aedes mosquito. Can also be transmitted sexually.

Precautions

Prevent mosquito bites (repellents, window screens), practice safe sex or abstinence if living in or returning from Zika-affected areas (especially important for pregnant women to prevent fetal transmission).

Cure & Treatment

No Specific Cure. The virus is usually mild and self-limiting. Treatment is supportive: rest, plenty of fluids, and Acetaminophen to relieve fever and joint pain.
